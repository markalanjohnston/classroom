Lesson Snapshot
1.1 1.3
  • Course: Fundamentals of Computer Science (9th Grade)
  • Topic: Conditional Statements — if / elif / else
  • Platform: CMU Academy (Unit 4.1)
  • Lesson Length: 90 minutes
  • Where This Fits: Unit 4 builds on Unit 3 variables and program flow; conditionals are the gateway to loops (Unit 5) and all branching logic students will use for the rest of the course

Students explore how computer programs make decisions — predicting outcomes from code, physically modeling conditional logic, designing original algorithms, and implementing a working program. Everyday real-world scenarios (weather decisions, clothing choices, traffic lights) anchor abstract syntax in familiar experience throughout the lesson.

Standards Alignment & Curriculum Fit
1.1

This lesson is aligned to the following Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS):

  • CS TEKS §126.33(c)(3)(A): Demonstrate the use of programming concepts including variables, expressions, sequences, selection, and iteration
  • CS TEKS §126.33(c)(3)(B): Implement programs that use selection (conditional statements) to make decisions

Objectives are rigorous, measurable, and logically sequenced to the lesson's goal. Students progress from recognizing conditional syntax (understand) to predicting program output (apply) to diagnosing logical errors (analyze) to generating original real-world algorithms (evaluate/create).

Vertical alignment note: This lesson builds directly on Unit 3 (variables, basic program flow) and establishes the conditional logic foundation required for Unit 5 (loops) and all subsequent project-based work in the course. Students are explicitly shown how today's concepts fit within this broader structure.

Technology (CMU Academy) is integrated throughout — not as a supplemental tool, but as the primary environment where students encounter, test, and implement all examples. Students run live code to verify predictions and immediately observe the effect of their changes.

Instructional Structure
1.1 1.4

The lesson follows a gradual release model — a research-based framework that moves from teacher-led modeling to collaborative practice to fully independent application. Each phase is logically sequenced and provides appropriate time for student work, reflection, and lesson closure.

Phase Activity Time
Activate Warm-up hook — partner discussion, call on pre-warned students 5 min
I Do Follow Along Part 1 — teacher models if / else / elif in CMU Academy with live code 10 min
Check Checkpoint 1 — ABCD cards; pacing decision made here 3 min
We Do Kinesthetic activity — students physically become the conditional logic 10 min
I Do Follow Along Part 2 — multiple elif branches, cross-axis logic 10 min
Check + Discuss Checkpoint 2 + Think–Pair–Share + ABCD Formative Part 1 10 min
We Do Human Algorithm — groups design an original conditional algorithm 7 min
You Do Stoplight Coding Exercise — independent implementation 20 min
Check ABCD Check Part 2 3 min
Extend (if time) Guessing Game and/or Bug Finder optional activities 5–8 min
Close Exit ticket: Quick Write + Confidence Survey (Fundamental 5) 5 min

An enriching extension (Early Finisher Challenge: automating the stoplight with onStep() / sleep()) is available for students who reach mastery early. This extends beyond the lesson objective while remaining within the discipline.

What Students Will Be Doing
2.3 3.3
  • Discussing a conceptual warm-up question with a partner before any instruction begins
  • Predicting code output before running it — forming and testing hypotheses in real time
  • Responding to formative questions using ABCD cards (all students respond simultaneously)
  • Physically modeling conditional logic through a whole-class kinesthetic activity
  • Explaining reasoning to a partner in Think–Pair–Share discussions
  • Generating an original real-world conditional algorithm in small groups
  • Implementing a working conditional program independently (Stoplight exercise)
  • Diagnosing a logical error in broken code (Bug Finder — if time permits)
  • Reflecting in writing on conceptual understanding and self-assessing confidence
Student voice and reasoning are central: At every major transition, students are asked why — not just what the answer is, but why the logic works the way it does. Think–Pair–Share and group discussion require students to articulate reasoning, not just select answers.

Anticipated Misconceptions & How They Are Addressed
2.2 2.3

The lesson anticipates and proactively addresses the three most common conceptual pitfalls for this topic. Misconceptions are not simply corrected — they are used as teaching moments to deepen understanding.

Misconception How It Is Addressed
"if and elif do the same thing" — students often assume they are interchangeable Checkpoint 1 asks students to predict what happens when elif is replaced with if, then they run the code to verify. ABCD Formative Q2 revisits this directly. Bug Finder presents broken code where if is incorrectly used in place of elif, and students must identify and explain the error.
"All conditions are always checked" — students may not understand that elif short-circuits once a match is found The kinesthetic activity makes this physically tangible: students who match the IF condition stop there and do not evaluate ELIF. The debrief question — "Why didn't the computer check the second condition for some of you?" — targets this directly.
"else always runs" — students may believe else executes regardless of other conditions ABCD Check Part 2 directly assesses this. Earlier code examples (only 2–3 of four canvas quadrants produce circles) provide a concrete observable counterexample before the question is posed.
Prediction-before-execution is used throughout: students commit to an answer via ABCD cards before the code is run. This creates a self-correcting habit and surfaces misconceptions at the moment they are most teachable.

Student Self-Assessment & Confidence Monitoring
1.2 2.1

Several structures in the lesson invite students to evaluate their own understanding and build awareness of their strengths and areas for growth:

  • Predict-before-run: Before executing each code example, students commit to a prediction. When output differs from the prediction, students immediately identify a misconception to address — without being told.
  • Two-prompt exit ticket: Students choose between explaining why programmers use elif (conceptual) or generating an original real-world example (applied). This allows students to self-select the form of demonstration that best reflects their confidence and understanding.
  • Confidence survey (Fundamental 5): Students rate their own confidence at the close of the lesson, building metacognitive awareness and giving the teacher data beyond the written response.
Student Engagement Strategies
3.3 2.4

Strategies are varied throughout the lesson to address multiple learning modalities and maintain momentum across 90 minutes:

  • Partner discussion — verbal processing at two points (warm-up, Bug Finder)
  • Whole-class simultaneous response — ABCD cards hold all students accountable, not just those called on
  • Kinesthetic movement — students physically model conditional logic using their own attributes (birth month, shoe color), making the concept personal
  • Live, interactive coding — examples are run in real time; students watch outcomes they predicted become visible on screen
  • Small group collaboration — groups generate an original algorithm and share with the class
  • Independent coding — students implement conditionals from scratch in the Stoplight exercise
  • Written reflection — exit ticket consolidates learning through composition
Relevance by design: Every engagement activity uses a context students already know — the weather, their own clothing, a traffic light, a guessing game. Content is connected to students' interests, experiences, and daily life throughout, not just at the opening.
Differentiation & Support
2.4

The lesson uses a wide variety of instructional strategies to address the individual needs of all learners:

  • Multiple modalities: visual (live code on screen), auditory (teacher explanation and whole-class discussion), kinesthetic (physical movement activity), and written (exit ticket)
  • Scaffolded complexity: Syntax is introduced incrementally — if/else first (already reviewed), then elif, then multiple elif branches, then logic combining multiple axes — each layer added only after the prior layer is confirmed
  • Real-world anchors before code: Every new construct is first encountered through a real-world analogy (weather, shoes) before abstract syntax is introduced, lowering the barrier for students who need concrete grounding
  • Structured group scaffolding: The Human Algorithm prompt provides a clear scaffold (IF ___ / ELIF ___ / ELSE ___) while leaving the content open, allowing all ability levels to participate productively
  • Partner support: Verbal processors have two structured partner discussion opportunities before independent work
  • Early finisher challenge: Students who complete the Stoplight exercise early are directed to research and apply onStep() and sleep() independently — an extension requiring self-directed inquiry into new documentation
  • Teacher circulation and individual feedback: During the 20-minute coding exercise, the teacher circulates and provides specific, individual academic feedback to each student
Lesson Closure
2.1 1.2

Closure is structured as a Fundamental 5 Quick Write — students write 3–4 sentences responding to one of two prompts at the evaluative/synthesis level:

  • Why do programmers use elif instead of multiple if statements?
  • Describe a real-world situation that could be represented with if, elif, and else.

The dual-prompt design allows students to demonstrate understanding in the form that best reflects their learning. Students also complete a brief confidence survey, prompting them to self-evaluate mastery and giving the teacher data on both understanding and affect.

Closure loops back to the Essential Question stated at the lesson's opening ("How can a computer make decisions based on conditions?"), giving the lesson a complete arc from question to answer.
