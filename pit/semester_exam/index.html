PIT · SEMESTER EXAM

Career Application & Year-One Review

A 180-minute professional simulation: pick a job, write a cover letter, build a portfolio site, and complete a year-one self-review.

180-minute block
100 points
2 deliverables (webpage + PDF)
Individual work

The Scenario

This semester exam is a professional simulation in three layered roles. You will:

  1. Apply for one of fifteen real-looking entry-level job postings — chosen from a wide range of industries, not just IT.
  2. Get the job (we’ll simulate it) and build a small portfolio website on the classroom Gitea server that an employer could actually look at.
  3. Fast-forward one year. Conduct a year-one self-review the way real employees do: rate yourself on the soft skills every job uses, reflect on what you learned technically, and set a goal for year two.

The goal is not to predict your career — it’s to demonstrate the employability skills (TEKS §130.312(c)(1) and (c)(2)) that this course is built around. Pick a posting that interests you, even if it’s not what you’ll actually do after graduation.

Your Role Today

You are wearing three hats over the next 180 minutes: job applicant, new hire building a professional web presence, and year-one employee reflecting on their first year on the job.

Treat your work the way a real employer would read it. Cover letters with typos get rejected. Portfolio sites that don’t load are invisible. Self-reviews that just say “I’m great at everything” aren’t taken seriously by managers in the real world — and they won’t score well here either.

PART 1

Cover Letter

35 min · 20 pts
Objective.

Write a one-page cover letter tailored to the specific posting you chose. It should sound like you, not a template, and it should reference the actual job.

FORMAT Required structure

A professional cover letter has four parts. Yours must include all four.

  1. Header. Your name, phone, email, the date, and the employer’s name and address (use the company name from the posting; you can use El Paso, TX as the address).
  2. Opening paragraph (2–3 sentences). Name the specific position you’re applying for, where you saw the posting (you can write “your job posting on the company careers page”), and a one-sentence hook on why you’re a fit.
  3. Body (1–2 paragraphs). Tie your actual coursework, projects, and skills to the responsibilities listed in the posting. Be specific. “In my PIT class I used Git daily to push code to a classroom server” beats “I am good with computers.”
  4. Closing (2–3 sentences). Thank the reader, state your availability for an interview, and include a professional sign-off.

QUALITY What graders look for

  • Tailored, not generic. If your cover letter would work for any of the 15 postings, it’s too generic.
  • Specific evidence. Reference at least two concrete things from this semester (assignments, tools, projects) that map to the posting.
  • Professional tone. No slang, no emojis, no exclamation points stacked together.
  • Clean formatting. Consistent font, readable size (11–12 pt), one page, properly aligned.
  • Proofread. Typos in a cover letter are typically the first reason a real recruiter rejects a candidate.
AI policy. You may use AI to brainstorm or critique a draft, but the final words must be yours and must accurately describe your work. Submitting an AI-generated cover letter with details you didn’t actually do is academic dishonesty.

PART 3

Year-One Soft-Skills Self-Review

30 min · 30 pts
Objective.

Imagine it’s a year from now. You got the job from Part 1. You’ve been on the job for twelve months. It’s time for your first annual self-review. Many real companies make employees write theirs first — that’s what you’re doing here.

Rate yourself on five soft-skill categories. Real performance reviews don’t go well when the employee just says “5 across the board” with no evidence — managers see right through it. Yours won’t score well that way either.

How this is graded. The goal is honest, evidence-based self-evaluation. Mid-range ratings (2–4) with concrete, specific examples score highest. Ratings at the extreme high end (5) may be cross-referenced against attendance records, assignment submissions, and observed classroom behavior — so make sure the evidence supports the rating.

For each of the five categories below, write:

  • A self-rating on a 1–5 scale (1 = needs significant improvement, 3 = meets expectations, 5 = consistently exceeds expectations)
  • A specific example from this semester that supports the rating
  • One concrete way you would improve (or sustain) this skill in year two

1 / 5 Punctuality & Attendance

Showing up on time, every day, is the foundation of professional reliability. Tardies and unexcused absences in this class are a direct preview of what those behaviors look like at a real job.

Prompt: How consistently did you arrive on time, ready to work? Use specific evidence (attendance record, tardies, how you handled days when you ran late).

2 / 5 Teamwork & Collaboration

Every job listed in Part 1 has a collaboration component — peer review, handoffs, paired work, classroom-to-team transitions.

Prompt: How did you contribute when working with a partner or in a group this semester (e.g., the peer-review block of the Gitea lesson, paired troubleshooting in the Python Deployment lab)? Give one specific example where you either lifted the team up or fell short.

3 / 5 Communication (Written & Verbal)

Most of your professional communication will be written — email, tickets, commit messages, documentation. Your verbal communication shows up when you ask for help or present work.

Prompt: How clearly did you communicate this semester? Use one written example (email, commit message, lab report, etc.) and one verbal example (how you asked for help, how you explained your work).

4 / 5 Professional Conduct & Workplace Standards

This category includes dress, language, phone use, lab cleanliness, and respect for the space and the people in it.

Prompt: How well did you meet the professional standards expected in this classroom (and that would be expected in any real workplace)? Specifically address phone use, language, and how you treated equipment and classmates.

5 / 5 Adaptability & Response to Feedback

Receiving feedback gracefully and applying it is one of the most-watched behaviors in any first-year employee’s review.

Prompt: Describe a piece of feedback you received this semester — from a teacher, classmate, or anyone — and how you applied it. Use a specific example. What did you do differently afterward? If you didn’t apply the feedback, be honest about why.

Reflection format. Each category should be roughly 4–6 sentences total. Use complete sentences. No bullet fragments. Treat this the way you’d want a real manager to read it.

(Hint: every job above is downstream of someone who documents carefully, follows a process, and handles failure gracefully.)

PART 5

Future Goal

10 min · 15 pts
Objective.

Set one actionable career-development goal for the next twelve months. The goal should be specific, realistic, and clearly tied to the posting you chose.

Write a single paragraph (5–8 sentences) covering:

  • The goal itself. A specific certification, course, internship, project, license step, or experience — not “get better at communication.”
  • Why this goal. Tie it directly to the responsibilities or requirements of the posting.
  • How you’d pursue it. One or two concrete steps (sign up by date X, complete by date Y).
  • How you’d know you achieved it. What would be different about you a year from now?
Examples of well-scoped goals. “Pass the CompTIA A+ Core 1 exam by December 2026,” “Complete a 200-hour CNA-to-LVN bridge program,” “Build and ship a personal portfolio site in React,” “Earn the QuickBooks ProAdvisor certification.”
