Session 1: From Passion → Problem → Possibility

Session Length: 180 minutes
Focus: Identifying meaningful problems worth solving through information technology

Session Overview

In this session, students begin the practicum experience by exploring areas of personal interest and identifying real-world problems that could be addressed through technology. Rather than starting with a pre-assigned project or jumping directly into coding, students focus on problem discovery, research, and professional communication.

By the end of this session, each student will have identified at least one well-defined problem and documented their thinking in a form suitable for a professional portfolio.

Session Activities

1

Orientation & Expectations

Time: 0–15 minutes Format: Instructor-led discussion

Students are introduced to how Practicum in Information Technology operates and how it differs from traditional classroom instruction.

Key ideas emphasized:

  • Practicum projects are not random or purely technical
  • Real IT work begins with identifying meaningful problems
  • Planning, design, and justification are just as important as implementation
  • Students do not need to code every part of a solution to demonstrate mastery

Students are shown examples of acceptable project domains, such as:

  • Music organization or recommendation systems
  • Gaming tools (e.g., D&D calculators, stat trackers, digital assistants)
  • AI-assisted tools or chatbots
  • School or workflow improvement tools
  • Hobby- or interest-based digital products or services
2

Research & Exploration (Individual Work)

Time: 15–45 minutes Format: Independent research

Students explore an area they are interested in and research existing tools, systems, or services related to that area. The goal is not to design a solution yet, but to understand what already exists and where gaps or frustrations may be present.

Guiding questions include:

  • What tools or systems already exist in this space?
  • Who uses them?
  • What complaints, limitations, or frustrations appear frequently?
  • What tasks seem repetitive, inefficient, or confusing?

Students may use sources such as:

  • Online communities and forums
  • Product reviews
  • App store feedback
  • Video walkthroughs or demonstrations
Expected Output: Informal research notes identifying 3–5 potential pain points, supported by links or screenshots where appropriate.
3

Problem Discovery Writing

Time: 45–75 minutes Format: Independent writing

Students draft multiple possible problem statements based on their research and personal experience. At this stage, students are intentionally not allowed to propose solutions.

Each draft addresses:

  • Who experiences the problem
  • What makes the situation frustrating or inefficient
  • Why existing tools do not fully solve the issue
  • Where and when the problem occurs

This step ensures students focus on defining meaningful problems before attempting to solve them.

4

Instructor Feedback & Conferences

Time: 75–95 minutes Format: One-on-one check-ins

While students continue refining their ideas, the instructor meets briefly with each student to provide feedback and guidance.

Discussion questions may include:

  • Is this a true problem or simply a personal preference?
  • Who would realistically benefit from a solution?
  • Is the scope appropriate for a student-designed project?

Each student must receive approval on at least one problem statement before moving forward.

5

Peer Collaboration: Problem Pitching

Time: 95–115 minutes Format: Structured small-group discussion

Students rotate through small groups to present one of their problem ideas. Each student:

  • Describes the problem
  • Explains why it matters

Peers provide:

  • One clarification question
  • One constructive suggestion for improvement

At this stage, students do not form teams. The goal is to test ideas, not commit to them.

6

Refinement & Documentation

Time: 115–140 minutes Format: Independent writing

Students select one problem and refine it into a clear, concise, and defensible problem statement. This document becomes an initial artifact for the student's professional portfolio.

7

Collaboration & Team Exploration

Time: 140–165 minutes Format: Guided collaboration

Students may choose to:

  • Form a small team (1–2 partners) if interests and skills align
  • Continue working independently

Students who work independently are still required to participate in peer feedback and collaboration throughout the unit.

Students document:

  • Team members (if applicable)
  • Shared interests or goals
  • Why collaboration is beneficial for the project
8

Reflection & Exit

Time: 165–180 minutes Format: Written reflection

Students complete a short reflection addressing:

  • The problem they plan to pursue
  • Why the problem matters to them
  • Questions or uncertainties they still have

This reflection helps guide the next session and encourages metacognitive thinking.

Outcome of Session 1

By the end of this session, students will have:

  • Researched an interest area
  • Identified and refined a real-world problem
  • Received peer and instructor feedback
  • Begun building a professional project portfolio

Session 1 Assignment: Problem Identification & Justification

Assignment Type Understanding
Grade Weight 4.5% of Semester Grade
Associated Session Session 1

Assignment Overview

In this assignment, students demonstrate their understanding of how information technology professionals identify meaningful problems before designing solutions. Students will document a real-world problem connected to an area of personal interest and justify why it is worth solving.

This assignment focuses on thinking, research, and communication, not coding or building.

Student Task

Students will submit a written Problem Identification Document that includes research, analysis, and reflection. This document serves as an early artifact for the student's professional portfolio.

Required Deliverable

A single document (digital) containing the following sections:

1. Problem Description

  • Clearly describe one real-world problem
  • Identify who experiences the problem
  • Explain when and where the problem occurs

2. Evidence & Research

  • Summarize what currently exists related to this problem
  • Identify specific limitations, frustrations, or gaps
  • Include evidence such as:
    • links
    • screenshots
    • quotes from reviews or forums

3. Problem Significance

  • Explain why this problem matters
  • Describe who would benefit from a better solution
  • Justify why the problem is worth time and effort to address

4. Reflection

  • Explain why this problem is personally interesting or meaningful
  • Identify any remaining questions or uncertainties
Important: Students should not propose a solution in this assignment. The focus is on problem identification and justification only.

Submission Instructions

Refer to the online Learning Management System (e.g., Schoology) for:

  • specific due dates
  • submission format
  • file naming requirements

Late or incomplete submissions are subject to course policies outlined in the LMS.

Assessment Rubric

Criteria Exemplary (4) Proficient (3) Developing (2) Beginning (1)
Problem Clarity Problem is clearly defined, specific, and easy to understand Problem is clear but may lack specificity Problem is vague or overly broad Problem is unclear or poorly defined
Research & Evidence Strong evidence from multiple relevant sources supports the problem Some relevant evidence is provided Minimal or weak evidence Little to no evidence included
Problem Significance Clearly explains why the problem matters and who is affected Explains importance with limited depth Importance is mentioned but not justified Significance is unclear or missing
Reflection & Professional Communication Thoughtful reflection; writing is clear and professional Reflection present; writing mostly clear Reflection limited or writing unclear Reflection missing or poorly written
Score Conversion:
  • 16–15 = Exemplary
  • 14–12 = Proficient
  • 11–9 = Developing
  • 8 or below = Beginning

TEKS Alignment

This assignment directly supports:

  • Research and problem identification
  • Evaluation of existing systems and processes
  • Professional documentation and portfolio development

Purpose of This Assignment

This assessment ensures students understand that effective IT solutions begin with well-defined problems, informed by research and user needs. The skills demonstrated here will be used repeatedly throughout the course and extended in later sessions.
