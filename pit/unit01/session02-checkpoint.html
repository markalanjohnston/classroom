Checkpoint Session: Project Reality Check & Catch-Up

Shark Tank Problem Analysis

Session Length: 180 minutes
Theme: Real problems first. Clear problems win.

Big Idea

If you cannot clearly explain the problem and prove it is real, you are likely building a solution that nobody needs.

Session Overview

This checkpoint session is intentionally placed between Session 2 and Session 3 to ensure students have a strong foundation before beginning formal project planning. Students will analyze Shark Tank pitches as case studies to identify what happens when entrepreneurs are unclear about the problem, solving a weak or nonexistent problem, or proposing a solution that does not match a real need.

Students will then apply those lessons directly to their own projects by revising their problem statements, strengthening evidence, and making a decision to proceed, pivot, or scale down their project scope. Time is also built in for students who need to catch up on Session 1 and Session 2 work.

This session supports students who are behind while still providing meaningful progress for students who are on track.

Checkpoint Session Goals

By the end of this session, students will:

  • Identify common reasons project ideas fail early (unclear problem, weak evidence, unclear user, already-solved problem, unrealistic scope)
  • Strengthen their own problem statement using clearer language, specific users, and stronger evidence
  • Catch up on missing or incomplete work from Session 1 and Session 2
  • Decide on a project path: Proceed, Pivot, or Scale Down
  • Leave with a clearer, more defensible foundation before moving into constraints, scope, and MVP planning (Session 3)

High-Level Structure

This session follows a practical professional workflow:

  • Analyze examples of weak problem definition (case studies)
  • Identify patterns of failure and success
  • Apply those patterns to improve project foundations
  • Course-correct early to avoid wasting time building the wrong thing

Checkpoint Session Breakdown (180 Minutes)

1

Quick Write: Problem Clarity Check

Time: 0–10 minutes Format: Individual writing

Students write a one-sentence problem statement and answer two follow-up questions:

  • Who experiences this problem?
  • What evidence shows it is real?

Purpose: Reveal weak problem definitions early and prepare students for revision.

2

Independent Case Study: Shark Tank Problem Analysis

Time: 10–60 minutes Format: Individual video analysis TEKS: 4(A), 5(A)

Students watch selected Shark Tank pitches and complete a structured analysis in a typed document.

Students focus on:

  • What problem is being claimed?
  • Who is the user?
  • What evidence supports the problem?
  • What questions exposed weaknesses?
  • What lesson applies to my project?

This analysis is completed individually so students can work at their own pace and so absent students can complete it asynchronously.

Deliverable: Completed Shark Tank Problem Analysis document (typed, submitted as PDF)
3

Discussion: Patterns That Sink Projects

Time: 60–80 minutes Format: Small groups → Class debrief TEKS: 1(F), 1(I)

Students share observations in small groups and then debrief as a class.

Discussion centers on patterns such as:

  • Vague or overly broad problems
  • No clear target user
  • Lack of evidence or validation
  • "Already solved" ideas with no differentiation
  • Mismatch between problem severity and solution complexity
  • Unrealistic scope for time and resources
4

Apply to Your Project: Revision and Improvement

Time: 80–110 minutes Format: Individual revision work TEKS: 4(C), 5(B)

Students revise their own problem statement and supporting evidence using the patterns identified.

Key targets for revision:

  • Make the problem specific and measurable
  • Identify a real target user
  • Strengthen evidence from research, reviews, or user feedback
  • Clarify the gap in existing solutions
5

Catch-Up Time + Instructor Feedback Conferences

Time: 110–160 minutes Format: Individual work + conferences TEKS: 1(H), 6(F)

Students complete missing Session 1–2 work as needed while the teacher meets with students for quick conferences.

Conference goal: Help each student make a practical decision about what to do next.

Students select one project path:

  • Proceed: Problem is strong and plan is realistic
  • Pivot: Keep the general domain, change the problem or approach
  • Scale Down: Keep the core idea, reduce scope to a realistic portfolio artifact
6

Exit Reflection: Project Path Decision

Time: 160–180 minutes Format: Individual writing TEKS: 7(A)

Students submit a short reflection summarizing:

  • Their revised problem statement
  • Their strongest evidence
  • Their decision (Proceed / Pivot / Scale Down)
  • Their next step heading into Session 3
Deliverable: Exit reflection document (typed, submitted)

Checkpoint Session Deliverables

By the end of this checkpoint session, students will produce:

  • A completed Shark Tank Problem Analysis document (typed and submitted as a PDF)
  • A revised problem statement with clearer user and stronger evidence
  • A written decision: Proceed, Pivot, or Scale Down
  • Catch-up completion of missing Session 1 and/or Session 2 artifacts (if needed)

Notes for Students and Parents

This checkpoint session is designed to improve project quality. It is normal in real-world technology work to realize early that an idea is unclear, unrealistic, or already solved. Making that realization early and adjusting course is a sign of strong thinking, not failure.

Students are not expected to have perfect project ideas at this point. They are expected to use evidence, feedback, and reflection to make their project stronger before moving into formal planning.

Checkpoint Session Outcomes

By the end of this checkpoint session, students should have:

  • Stronger problem statements grounded in evidence
  • Understanding of common failure patterns in project definition
  • A clear, justified decision about their project direction
  • Completed foundation work from Sessions 1–2
  • Readiness to proceed into Session 3 (constraints and MVP planning)
