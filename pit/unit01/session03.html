Session 3: Constraints, Scope, and MVP Definition

Session Length: 180 minutes
Format: Self-Guided
Focus: Identifying constraints and defining a realistic, achievable MVP

Session 3 Template Document

Complete all written responses in your copy of this template document.

Open Template in Google Docs Click File → Make a copy to edit in Google Docs, or File → Download to edit in Word.

Session Overview

Today you will turn your project idea into a real, executable plan.

Up to this point, you have:

  • identified a meaningful problem
  • researched existing solutions
  • proposed an initial approach

In this session, you will slow down and think critically about limits, scope, and what is actually buildable in a short amount of time. The goal is to leave class with a plan that allows you to immediately begin prototyping or creating a mock-up in the next sessions.

This session is intentionally self-guided. You are responsible for pacing yourself, following instructions carefully, and producing the required written artifacts.

How to Work Today

  • Work independently or with your approved team
  • Follow each block in order
  • Complete all writing in your Session 3 template document
  • If you finish early, complete the Finished Early? section
  • Idle time is not acceptable — there is more than enough work to fill the entire session

Session Blocks

1

Warm-Up Reflection

10 minutes

In your template document, respond to the following prompts:

  1. List five reasons your current project idea might fail.
  2. Identify the biggest unknown in your plan right now.

Be honest. This exercise is meant to surface risks early, not punish creativity.

2

Reading and Response: Prioritization

25 minutes
Read:

Prioritization with MoSCoW
https://medium.com/@uniyalmeenakshi/prioritization-with-moscow-8025fdebb41a

Write:
  • A brief summary of the MoSCoW method in your own words
  • An explanation of how MoSCoW applies to your project
Then create an initial MoSCoW list for your project:
  • Must Have
  • Should Have
  • Could Have
  • Won't Have

Focus on realism, not ambition.

3

Feature Brainstorm

20 minutes

List every feature you can imagine for your project. Do not filter yet.

After brainstorming, sort each feature into the MoSCoW categories.

This step helps separate:

  • essential functionality
  • optional enhancements
  • ideas that must be intentionally excluded
4

Reading and Response: MVP Thinking

35 minutes
Read:

Building a Minimum Viable Product? You're Probably Doing it Wrong
Harvard Business Review
https://hbr.org/2013/09/building-a-minimum-viable-prod

Write:
  • The difference between a validating MVP and an invalidating MVP
  • Which type is more appropriate for your project and why
  • The risks of choosing the wrong type of MVP

Your response should demonstrate understanding, not just restate the article.

5

Constraints Mapping

25 minutes

Every project has limits. Identify yours clearly.

In your template, document constraints in the following categories:

Constraint Categories:
  • Time
  • Skills
  • Tools or technology
  • Data availability
  • Users
  • Security and privacy
  • Feasibility

If you ignore constraints now, they will stop you later.

6

Scope Definition

20 minutes

Define the boundaries of Version 1 of your project.

Create two lists:

INCLUDED in Version 1

What will exist in your prototype or mock-up

NOT INCLUDED in Version 1

Features you are intentionally leaving out

If you cannot clearly state what you are not building, your scope is too large.

7

Formal MVP Definition

25 minutes

Define your MVP precisely.

You must write:

  1. A one-paragraph description of your MVP
  2. A bullet list of exact functions it will include
  3. A clear explanation of how success will be measured
Your MVP must be:
  • specific
  • measurable
  • realistic
  • achievable within two class periods
8

Work Plan for Upcoming Class Meetings

20 minutes

You will have dedicated build time during the next class meeting and the meeting after next to complete your prototype or mock-up.

Your task is to create a clear, realistic work plan for both sessions.

Next Class Meeting – Work Plan

Describe how you will use the next class meeting productively.

Your plan must include:

  • specific tasks you will complete
  • estimated time for each task
  • tools or resources needed
  • individual responsibilities (if working in a team)

Meeting After Next – Work Plan

Describe how you will continue or complete your prototype during the meeting after next.

Your plan must include:

  • remaining tasks
  • testing or refinement steps
  • documentation or visuals to complete
  • what "done" looks like by the end of this session

Important:

These plans must reflect the constraints and MVP you defined earlier. If your plan requires more time than two class periods, your scope is too large and must be revised.

9

Peer Reality Check

15 minutes

Share your plan with another student or team.

They must review your work and respond to:

  • Is the scope realistic?
  • Is the MVP too large?
  • Are constraints clearly addressed?
  • Is the timeline believable?

Revise your plan based on feedback.

10

Final Submission Artifact

End of class

Before the end of class, submit one completed document containing:

  • MoSCoW prioritization
  • Constraints analysis
  • Scope definition
  • Formal MVP definition
  • Two-meeting work plan

This will be assessed as an Understanding grade.

Finished Early?

If you complete all required sections early, create visual materials to support Session 4.

Choose at least two of the following:

  • system flowchart
  • interface wireframes
  • screen mockups
  • user interaction storyboard
  • hardware or software diagrams
  • branding elements (logo, color palette, icons)
  • draft presentation slides

For each visual artifact, include a short caption explaining:

  • what it represents
  • how it connects to your MVP

Exit Requirement

Before leaving class, include this sentence in your document:

"My MVP is __________."

Final Reminder

Strong planning now makes building easier later.
Vague plans lead to wasted build time.

Take this session seriously — it sets the foundation for everything that follows.
